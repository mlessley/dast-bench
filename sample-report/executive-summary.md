# Executive Summary

## Overview

Three candidates were evaluated against a 29-criterion taxonomy (expanded
from an initial 12-criterion draft to reflect enterprise financial-services
evaluation depth — compliance mapping, business-logic testing, deployment/
data governance, and GRC-oriented reporting). **OWASP ZAP** and **Nuclei**
were carried through to hands-on testing (`evaluated`); **StackHawk** was
rejected before that phase, on practical grounds rather than merit.

| Vendor | Weighted Total | Status |
|---|---|---|
| StackHawk | **3.61** | rejected (practical access reasons — see below) |
| Nuclei | 3.20 | evaluated |
| OWASP ZAP | 3.20 | evaluated |

The criteria expansion changed the picture meaningfully: StackHawk pulled
further ahead of the two hands-on-tested tools, and ZAP/Nuclei — previously
0.5 points apart — are now in a dead tie, each strong in different places.

## StackHawk — highest score, but not hands-on tested

StackHawk's paper score improved substantially once enterprise/regulated-
industry criteria entered the taxonomy. Three capabilities stood out as
genuinely differentiated, not just plausible-sounding marketing:
**Intelligent Business Logic Testing** (4.5/5) — a purpose-built,
multi-user-perspective testing mode designed specifically to catch
authorization flaws (BOLA-style), which neither ZAP nor Nuclei has an
equivalent for; explicit, well-documented **compliance mapping** to
PCI-DSS, HIPAA, and SOC 2 (4.5/5), including a dedicated PCI DSS v4.0.1
guide; and a real **finding-lifecycle workflow** (4/5) — findings can be
marked Assigned, Risk Accepted, or False Positive, tied directly to Jira,
which is a genuine governance capability the other two candidates lack
entirely.

**This is still excluded from hands-on testing for the same practical
reason as before**: it requires a paid SaaS account/API key not available
for this evaluation. With the gap between StackHawk and the two
hands-on-tested tools now wider (3.61 vs. 3.20), this is more worth
revisiting — if a trial account becomes available, StackHawk is the
strongest single candidate for onboarding to real hands-on testing next,
not just a paper leader by a small margin.

## OWASP ZAP — tied for second, strongest on deployment control

ZAP's hands-on detection results are unchanged by this criteria expansion
(the two hands-on-refined criteria, `detection-accuracy`/
`false-positive-rate`, weren't touched): 11/15 (73%) known vulnerabilities
detected across both benchmark targets, 5 false positives, against ground
truth rebuilt from real observed scan output. Its standout new-criteria
result is **Deployment Model & Data Residency** (5/5) — being fully
self-hostable by nature (docker/desktop/CLI, not a SaaS product at all),
it offers complete data control by default, which matters for a
regulated-industry buyer that may be unable to send production traffic to
external infrastructure. It's weakest on the newly-added governance-style
criteria — **Vulnerability Status Management** and **Multi-Tenancy & Access
Control** both scored 1/5 — reflecting honestly that ZAP is a scanning
engine, not a full AppSec governance platform, and would need to be paired
with separate tooling (a ticketing system, a GRC platform) for those
functions rather than providing them natively.

## Nuclei — tied for second, strongest on discovery and integration

Nuclei's hands-on detection results are also unchanged: 4/15 (27%)
detected, 17 false positives (a coarse count — see the caveat below).
Where Nuclei pulled real weight in the expanded criteria: its parent
platform, ProjectDiscovery's cloud offering, is a genuine
attack-surface-management product, earning a 5/5 on **Asset Discovery &
Inventory Tracking** — an RSAC 2025 Innovation Sandbox finalist
specifically for this — and native, documented integrations to Splunk and
Elasticsearch (**SIEM/SOAR Integration**, 4.5/5) that neither ZAP nor
StackHawk matches. Its weakest results are on governance-style criteria
similar to ZAP (**Vulnerability Status Management** at 2/5) and on
criteria where its signature-matching design is a structural mismatch
(**Business Logic & Workflow Detection**, 2/5; **Sensitive Financial Data
Exposure Detection**, 2/5) — not a flaw in the tool, but a reminder that
Nuclei's strength (known-CVE/exposure template matching) is a different
shape of capability than what a bank's actual risk profile most needs to
cover.

## Trade-offs worth flagging

- **False-positive-rate's hands-on number is coarser than it looks.** It
  counts any finding not on the ground-truth list as a false positive,
  including legitimate low-value fingerprinting — this affects Nuclei's
  count more than ZAP's, since its default output includes far more
  fingerprinting-style findings. Its 2/5 score should be read with that in
  mind.
- **"Vendor Security Posture" scoring reflects what's independently
  verifiable, not what's marketed.** ZAP scored moderately (3/5) on the
  strength of Checkmarx's corporate backing and published (if
  request-gated) SOC 2 audits; StackHawk scored lower (2.5/5) despite
  extensive marketing about compliance, because that marketing is about
  helping *customers* comply with PCI-DSS/HIPAA/SOC2 — a different claim
  from StackHawk's own certification status, which isn't independently
  confirmed either way.
- **"Asset Discovery" measures two different things across candidates.**
  Nuclei's strength here is network-based (scanning IP ranges/domains for
  unknown, rogue assets). StackHawk's discovery mechanism is source-driven
  (mapping known GitHub/GitLab/Bitbucket/Azure repos to applications) —
  real, but answers a different question ("what apps do we have in version
  control" vs. "what's exposed to the internet that we don't know about").
  Worth keeping that distinction in mind rather than reading the numbers
  as directly comparable capabilities.

## Methodology note: ground truth was corrected mid-evaluation

The benchmark ground truth used for hands-on scoring (ZAP and Nuclei only —
this doesn't affect StackHawk's paper-only score) was rebuilt during this
evaluation after the original, documentation-curated list proved
unreachable by a realistic CI-budget scan, even after adding
authentication. The current ground truth reflects vulnerabilities
empirically demonstrated reachable by both tools within this scan
configuration, not an exhaustive list of every known vulnerability either
benchmark app contains. Read the detection-accuracy scores as "how well
did this tool find what's reachable in a realistic scan," not "how good is
this tool at finding every documented vulnerability in these apps."

## Criteria taxonomy note

The 12-criterion original draft was expanded to 29 criteria across 6
categories partway through this evaluation, informed by a real enterprise
DAST RFP matrix and the specific context that this evaluation is for a
Fortune 500 financial-services buyer. All three candidates have now been
scored against the full 29-criterion list; weights were re-derived from
scratch, not just proportionally adjusted, so weighted totals above are
directly comparable across all three vendors on equal footing.
